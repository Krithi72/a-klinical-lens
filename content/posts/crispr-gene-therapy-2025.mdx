## A Revolution in a Test Tube

In 2012, Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier published a paper in *Science* describing a system capable of cutting DNA at precise, programmable locations. It used a bacterial immune defence mechanism — CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) — repurposed as a molecular scalpel. The paper was titled: "A Programmable Dual-RNA–Guided DNA Endonuclease in Adaptive Bacterial Immunity."

Within a year, multiple groups had shown it could edit genes in human cells. By 2020, Doudna and Charpentier received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry. And by 2023, the first CRISPR-based medicine had received regulatory approval.

The speed of translation from basic science to clinical medicine is nearly unprecedented in modern biology.

## How CRISPR-Cas9 Works

The mechanism is elegantly simple at its core.

**CRISPR** sequences in bacterial genomes are derived from DNA of past viral invaders — a form of molecular memory. When the bacteria encounter the same virus again, CRISPR RNA guides the Cas9 protein to the matching sequence, which Cas9 then cuts.

In the repurposed therapeutic version:
1. A **guide RNA (gRNA)** is designed to match a target DNA sequence in the patient's genome
2. The gRNA-Cas9 complex is delivered to the target cells
3. Cas9 creates a **double-strand break** in the DNA at the target site
4. The cell's natural DNA repair mechanisms kick in:
   - **Non-homologous end joining (NHEJ)** — error-prone repair that can disrupt a gene
   - **Homology-directed repair (HDR)** — precise repair using a provided template, enabling specific corrections

This allows researchers to either **knock out** a gene (using NHEJ) or **correct** a mutation (using HDR).

## Casgevy: The First Approved CRISPR Therapy

In November 2023, both the FDA (US) and MHRA (UK) approved **Casgevy (exa-cel)**, developed by Vertex Pharmaceuticals and CRISPR Therapeutics, for sickle cell disease and transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia.

Both conditions involve haemoglobin gene mutations. Sickle cell disease causes red blood cells to deform into a characteristic crescent shape, causing painful vascular crises, organ damage, and shortened lifespan. Beta-thalassemia causes severe anaemia requiring regular blood transfusions.

**The approach**: Casgevy targets BCL11A, a gene that normally suppresses foetal haemoglobin (HbF) after birth. By disrupting BCL11A in the patient's haematopoietic stem cells, the treatment reactivates HbF production — providing a functional substitute for the defective adult haemoglobin.

**The results** from the Phase 3 trial were remarkable:
- 28 of 29 patients with sickle cell disease were **free from severe vaso-occlusive crises** for at least 12 months post-treatment
- 39 of 42 patients with beta-thalassemia achieved **transfusion independence**
- No serious adverse events attributed to the gene editing itself

This is genuinely transformative for patients who previously had no curative option other than bone marrow transplantation (limited by donor availability and immunological risk).

## Limitations and Open Questions

### Delivery

Getting CRISPR to the right cells is hard. For Casgevy, cells are extracted from the patient (*ex vivo* therapy), edited outside the body, and reinfused. This is logistically demanding, expensive, and requires specialist centres.

For diseases affecting the liver or brain — where *in vivo* editing would be required — delivery is far harder. Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) have shown promise for liver delivery; crossing the blood-brain barrier remains a major challenge.

### Off-target effects

CRISPR is precise but not perfectly so. The guide RNA can direct Cas9 to unintended sequences with partial complementarity. Off-target cuts could, in principle, disrupt tumour suppressor genes or other critical sequences. Long-term safety data are still accumulating.

New variants — **base editors** and **prime editors** — modify single nucleotides without creating double-strand breaks, potentially offering improved precision.

### Cost

Casgevy is priced at approximately **$2.2 million per patient** in the US. This raises profound equity questions. Sickle cell disease disproportionately affects people of African, South Asian, and Middle Eastern descent — populations that are often underrepresented in the healthcare systems of wealthy countries with the capacity to deliver such therapies.

The cost reflects the genuine complexity and investment in development. But at this price, it is inaccessible to the majority of the world's patients who need it.

### Germline editing

All approved CRISPR therapies to date involve **somatic** editing — changes to a patient's cells that are not heritable. Germline editing (modifying eggs, sperm, or embryos) would create heritable changes, affecting all future generations.

The case of He Jiankui, the Chinese researcher who in 2018 created the first gene-edited babies (claiming to confer HIV resistance), was condemned internationally. He was jailed. The scientific consensus remains that germline editing in humans is not ethically acceptable at present — the risks are poorly understood, the benefits uncertain, and the consent of the future child impossible.

## What's Next

The pipeline is extraordinary:

- **Duchenne muscular dystrophy** — base editors targeting exon skipping are in early trials
- **Transthyretin amyloidosis** — an *in vivo* CRISPR therapy (NTLA-2001) demonstrated impressive reductions in disease-causing protein after a single infusion
- **HIV** — CRISPR approaches targeting the viral reservoir are under investigation
- **Cancer immunotherapy** — CRISPR-edited CAR-T cells with enhanced persistence and tumour-killing ability are in trials
- **High cholesterol** — targeting PCSK9 to permanently reduce LDL cholesterol

## Reflections

Reading the original 2012 Doudna-Charpentier paper as a prospective medical student is a humbling experience. Twelve years later, a patient in the UK or US can receive a treatment derived directly from it and be cured of a lifelong genetic disease.

CRISPR illustrates something important about how medicine advances: it rarely proceeds linearly. It requires basic scientists asking questions about bacterial immunity, molecular biologists repurposing those findings, clinical trialists risking their reputations on unproven approaches, and regulators developing frameworks for genuinely novel therapies.

It also raises questions that are not scientific — about access, equity, and what we owe to future generations. These are questions that medicine cannot answer alone.

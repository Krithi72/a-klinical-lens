## Setting the Scene

By 2024, the NHS waiting list in England had exceeded **7.5 million** people. The average wait for elective treatment had stretched beyond 14 weeks. A&E performance — measured by the proportion of patients seen within four hours — had hit historic lows. Thousands of patients were waiting in corridors. Ambulance response times had hit record highs.

This is not an isolated crisis. It is the culmination of decisions made over more than a decade, filtered through a pandemic that exposed every pre-existing vulnerability.

## The Decade of Austerity

The Conservative-Liberal Democrat coalition government elected in 2010 inherited an economy scarred by the 2008 financial crisis. Its response was austerity: deep, sustained cuts to public spending.

The NHS received a **real-terms funding freeze** for much of the 2010s. In historic terms, NHS funding had grown at around 4% per year in real terms since its founding. From 2010 to 2018, that figure fell to around **1.1%** per year.

This might sound modest. But healthcare costs grow faster than general inflation — driven by an ageing population, expensive new treatments, and workforce costs. A real-terms freeze is, in effect, a cut relative to demand.

The King's Fund, Nuffield Trust, and Health Foundation — independent health policy organisations — have documented the consequences meticulously. By the late 2010s:

- **Capital investment** had been repeatedly raided to fund day-to-day operations
- **NHS estate** was crumbling — over 13,000 "high-risk" estates items in 2019
- **GP workforce** growth had stagnated despite rising demand
- **Mental health services** had absorbed disproportionate cuts despite rising need

## The Social Care Crisis and Its NHS Consequence

The NHS cannot be understood in isolation from **social care**. Social care — the system of support for elderly and disabled people — is funded separately from the NHS, largely by local authorities, and means-tested.

Local authority budgets were cut by around 40% in real terms between 2010 and 2020. Social care collapsed quietly while the NHS remained in the spotlight.

The consequence: **bed blocking**. Patients who were medically fit for discharge from hospital could not leave because there was nowhere suitable to go. This blocks hospital beds, backs up A&E, and forces ambulances to queue outside hospitals. It is a systemic failure dressed up as an NHS problem.

The NHS and social care are a single system in practice, divided by political convenience.

## The Workforce Crisis

The NHS has around **110,000 vacancies** (as of 2023–24 figures). The causes are structural:

**Pay**: NHS pay has fallen significantly in real terms over the past decade. Nursing pay, for example, was around 6% lower in 2021 than in 2010 in real terms. After inflation surged in 2022–23, real-terms pay cuts deepened dramatically — triggering the first coordinated nurses' strikes in NHS history.

**Burnout**: The pandemic was followed not by recovery but by continued pressure. Many experienced clinicians have left the profession; many more are considering it.

**Training pipeline**: The UK has historically trained insufficient numbers of doctors and nurses, relying heavily on international recruitment. Post-Brexit reductions in EU healthcare workers compounded existing gaps.

**International recruitment**: While the NHS has recruited significant numbers from South Asia and Africa, this raises its own ethical questions — we are recruiting staff from countries that can least afford to lose them.

## Private Sector: Solution or Problem?

A recurring political debate: should the private sector be brought in more extensively to reduce NHS waiting lists?

Proponents argue the private sector has spare capacity and can treat patients faster. Opponents note several things:

1. Private providers **cherry-pick** lucrative, straightforward cases — leaving the complex, expensive work to the NHS
2. Private sector growth depends on **NHS staff doing private sessions** — which may reduce NHS capacity
3. The evidence on cost-effectiveness of NHS outsourcing to the private sector is mixed at best

The 2022 Health and Care Act increased the role of independent sector providers in NHS commissioning. Whether this proves beneficial will take years to evaluate.

## What Politics Has to Do with It

The NHS is a deeply political institution. It was created by a Labour government against fierce Conservative opposition (Aneurin Bevan's proposal was described as a threat to freedom). Successive governments have rhetorically championed the NHS while making decisions that undermined it.

The political economy of healthcare reform is brutal. Reforms that save money in the long run — investment in prevention, primary care, social care — produce savings over decades. Politicians operate on electoral cycles of 4–5 years. This creates a structural bias towards under-investment in prevention.

The NHS is not failing because of a lack of talented, dedicated staff. It is failing because of chronically insufficient funding, a social care system in ruins, and a workforce stretched to breaking point by a decade of real-terms pay cuts.

These are political choices. They can be reversed by political choices. That requires the public to demand it — and to understand what has actually happened.

## Reflections

I find healthcare politics impossible to study without feeling something. The NHS represents one of the most remarkable collective achievements in British history. The idea that how much money you have should not determine whether you receive care when you are sick is — I believe — morally correct.

Seeing that principle slowly compromised by political decisions made for ideological rather than evidential reasons makes me angry. It also makes me more certain that medicine is the right path. You cannot separate clinical medicine from its political context. Understanding both is necessary.
